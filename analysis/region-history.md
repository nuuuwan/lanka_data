# How Sri Lanka's Administrative Regions Evolved

Sri Lanka's administrative geography reflects more than a century and a half of colonial administration, post-independence state-building, ethnic politics, and grassroots service delivery reform. From the original five provinces drawn up by British commissioners in 1833, the country today operates across nine provinces, 25 districts, and 340 Divisional Secretariat Divisions (DSDs) — a layered structure that expanded dramatically in the second half of the twentieth century.

---

## Part 1 — Provinces: From Five to Nine (1833–1987)

### The Colebrooke-Cameron Commission, 1833

Before British consolidation, the island was administered in fragments: the Dutch-legacy maritime littoral under the British Crown since 1796, and the independent Kandyan Kingdom (fallen in 1815) with its own separate system of Kandyan chiefs and headmen. The two administrative worlds were never truly unified until the Colebrooke-Cameron Commission of 1829–30, whose recommendations took effect in 1833.

The commission abolished the separate Kandyan administration and divided the entire island into **five provinces** under a single colonial government:

| Province | DCS Code | Territory |
|---|---|---|
| Western | LK-1 (also LK-6, LK-92) | Maritime Colombo, Chilaw, Puttalam; Kandyan Three Korales, Four Korales, Seven Korales |
| Central | LK-2 (LK-81) | Central Kandyan highlands |
| Southern | LK-3 (parts LK-82, LK-91) | Maritime Galle, Matara, Hambantota; Kandyan Uva and Saffragam |
| Northern | LK-4 (LK-71) | Maritime Jaffna, Mannar, Vanni; Kandyan Nuwara Kalawiya |
| Eastern | LK-5 (LK-72, parts LK-82) | Maritime Batticaloa, Trincomalee; Kandyan Bintenna and Tamankaduwa |

This rationalisation served the colonial economy: tea and rubber estates in the hills, coconut on the northwest coast, paddy in the north. Provinces were large enough for a British Agent to govern but too few to reflect the island's geographic and demographic diversity.

### Provincial Expansion, 1845–1889

Population growth, expanding plantation agriculture, and the practical difficulty of administering vast hinterlands from a single provincial capital drove four new provinces out of the existing five over the second half of the nineteenth century:

- **North Western Province (LK-6)** — Created 1845 from the northern fringe of the Western Province, covering the coconut triangle around Kurunegala and Puttalam.
- **North Central Province (LK-7)** — Created 1873 from parts of the Northern and Eastern Provinces, centred on the ancient irrigation civilisation around Anuradhapura.
- **Uva Province (LK-8)** — Created 1886 from parts of the Central, Eastern, and Southern Provinces; the dry-zone eastern escarpment of the central massif, centred on Badulla.
- **Sabaragamuwa Province (LK-9)** — Created 1889 from parts of the Southern and Western Provinces; the gem-mining and rubber country around Ratnapura and Kegalle.

By 1889 the island had nine provinces — the same nine that exist today — drawn essentially by ecological and economic logic under British imperialism.

### 1987: Provincial Councils and the Thirteenth Amendment

The nine provinces remained purely administrative units with no elected government until the **Thirteenth Amendment to the Constitution (November 1987)**, enacted in the context of the Indo-Sri Lanka Accord. The accord, brokered by Indian Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi and Sri Lankan President J. R. Jayewardene, was intended to address Tamil political grievances through devolution of power.

The **Provincial Councils Act No. 42 of 1987** created elected provincial legislatures with competence over education, health, local transport, agricultural services, and certain development functions. Seven councils commenced in early 1988. The Northern and Eastern Provinces were temporarily merged into a single unit (a key Tamil political demand), but the Supreme Court ruled in 2006 that the merger was unconstitutional; the two provinces were separated. The merged council had in any case functioned for only one year (1988) before dissolution.

The provincial structure thus acquired democratic legitimacy — and political salience — only in the late 1980s, more than 150 years after the boundaries themselves were drawn. The boundaries remain contested, particularly in the north and east, where demographic composition, war displacement, and settlement policy have all intersected with questions of which province particular territories should belong to.

## Part 2 — Districts: The 1950s Middle Tier

### The Administrative Districts Act, No. 22 of 1955

When Ceylon gained independence in 1948, the five-plus-four province system had no intermediate tier below it — a gap that hampered development planning, census administration, and the coordination of central government field services. The **Administrative Districts Act (No. 22 of 1955)** filled this gap, taking effect on 14 April 1955.

It created **20 administrative districts** as a new middle tier between provinces and the existing village-headman system. Each district was headed by a Government Agent (later renamed District Secretary), responsible for coordinating central government functions, land administration, disaster management, elections, and regional planning.

The 20 original districts, with their DCS codes:

| Province | Districts |
|---|---|
| Western | Colombo (LK-11), Kalutara (LK-13) |
| Central | Kandy (LK-21), Matale (LK-22), Nuwara Eliya (LK-23) |
| Southern | Galle (LK-31), Matara (LK-32), Hambantota (LK-33) |
| Northern | Jaffna (LK-41), Mannar (LK-42), Vavuniya (LK-43) |
| Eastern | Batticaloa (LK-51), Trincomalee (LK-53) |
| North Western | Kurunegala (LK-61), Puttalam (LK-62) |
| North Central | Anuradhapura (LK-71), Polonnaruwa (LK-72) |
| Uva | Badulla (LK-81) |
| Sabaragamuwa | Ratnapura (LK-91), Kegalle (LK-92) |

Note that the Western Province initially had only two districts (Colombo and Kalutara), and the Northern Province lacked a district in its Mullaitivu hinterland. These gaps would be addressed in subsequent decades.

*Why 1955?* The 1953 Census had revealed the inadequacy of existing administrative units for planning purposes. The new districts aligned closely with existing Revenue Divisional boundaries and were small enough for a Government Agent to know personally. The act also had a political dimension: a smaller, more numerous middle tier would dilute the influence of the existing provincial hierarchy and concentrate power in Colombo-appointed officers rather than elected (or informally influential) provincial figures.

### Five New Districts, 1959–1984

Between independence and 1984, five further districts were carved out of existing ones, driven by a mixture of geographic remoteness, demographic growth, and ethnic-political pressure:

| New District | Code | Year | Created From |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monaragala | LK-82 | 1959 | Badulla District |
| Ampara | LK-52 | 1961 | Batticaloa District |
| Gampaha | LK-12 | 1978 | Colombo District |
| Mullaitivu | LK-44 | 1978 | Parts of Jaffna, Mannar, and Vavuniya districts |
| Kilinochchi | LK-45 | 1984 | Jaffna District |

**Monaragala (1959)** was separated because Badulla District was geographically enormous; the dry-zone east of Uva Province had distinct agricultural and demographic conditions, and access to Badulla town was difficult. **Ampara (1961)** followed similar logic for the eastern dry zone south of Batticaloa, which had been receiving Sinhalese settlers under the post-independence Land Development Ordinance colonisation schemes — a fact that made the new district politically significant in the context of rising Sinhalese-Tamil tensions. **Gampaha (1978)** was split from the sprawling Colombo District as Greater Colombo's urbanisation had by then extended far into what is now the most densely populated district outside the capital itself.

**Mullaitivu (1978)** and **Kilinochchi (1984)** were both carved out of the Northern Province's Tamil-majority districts at a time of escalating ethnic conflict. Critics argued that the boundary adjustments — particularly the way Mullaitivu drew DSDs from Jaffna, Mannar, and Vavuniya — fragmented Tamil political geography. These two districts became focal points of the civil war that began in 1983.

No new districts have been created since 1984, fixing the count at **25**.

## Part 3 — Divisional Secretariat Divisions: Granular Governance Since 1992

### Origins: From Divisional Revenue Officers to Divisional Secretaries

Below the district level, Ceylon had long operated through **Revenue Divisions** headed by Divisional Revenue Officers (DROs), a colonial inheritance from the Agent system. These handled land records, revenue collection, and registration. After independence they multiplied to handle expanding welfare state functions.

The **Transfer of Powers (Divisional Secretaries) Act, No. 58 of 1992** formally renamed and reconstituted these units as **Divisional Secretariat Divisions (DSDs)**, with officers now called Divisional Secretaries reporting to the District Secretary. The new mandate was broader: land administration, social welfare programmes, permits, civil registration (births, deaths, marriages), national identity card issuance, and coordination of village-level activities through the Grama Niladhari network beneath them.

As of the current data, there are **340 DSDs** across the 25 districts.

### Why DSDs Grew

The expansion of DSDs reflects several forces:

1. **Population growth and urbanisation.** Sri Lanka's population roughly tripled between independence and the present, with urban sprawl around Colombo and densification across the wet zone placing greater service demands on divisional offices.
2. **Welfare state expansion.** Each DSD administers Samurdhi (poverty relief), drought relief, disaster compensation, and agricultural support. Larger divisions meant larger queues and more complaints; subdivision was a pragmatic response.
3. **The peace process and post-war reconstruction.** After the end of the civil war in 2009, several northern and eastern divisions were reorganised to align with resettlement patterns and to re-establish government presence in areas that had been outside state control.
4. **Political pressure.** Local politicians lobbied for new divisions — and the government post of Divisional Secretary that comes with them — as a patronage resource. A new division brings a government office, staff, and services, all of which benefit constituents and incumbents alike.

### The 2019 Gazette — A Major Restructuring

The most significant recent reorganisation came via **Extraordinary Gazette No. 2147/28 (2 October 2019)**, which added 17 new DSDs by splitting existing ones across three districts:

**Nuwara Eliya District (LK-23):** Five divisions split into ten, reflecting both population growth in the tea-estate belt and longstanding arguments that the tea-plantation Tamil community had been underserved by the existing large divisions centred on urban Nuwara Eliya.

| Before | After |
|---|---|
| Kothmale (LK-2303) | Kothmale East (LK-2303) + Kothmale West (LK-2302) |
| Hanguranketha (LK-2306) | Hanguranketha (LK-2306) + Mathurata (LK-2307) |
| Walapane (LK-2309) | Walapane (LK-2309) + Niladandahinna (LK-2310) |
| Nuwara-Eliya (LK-2312) | Nuwara-Eliya (LK-2312) + Thalawakelle (LK-2313) |
| Ambagamuwa (LK-2315) | Ambagamuwa Korale (LK-2315) + Norwood (LK-2314) |

**Galle District (LK-31):** Two divisions expanded to five, reflecting the rapid growth of coastal settlements near Hikkaduwa and along the Baddegama corridor.

**Ratnapura District (LK-91):** Balangoda split in two, acknowledging the district's expansion into gem-mining hinterland areas.

### The 2024 Gazette — Kalmunai

**Extraordinary Gazette No. 2393/12 (16 July 2024)** formally subordinated Kalmunai North (LK-5221) under the Kalmunai division. This change had deep roots: Kalmunai North was created in 1987 during the Provincial Councils devolution process as a division serving the Tamil sub-community within a Muslim-majority area, but was never granted the full land and financial powers of other DSDs. Subsequent attempts to give it full DSD status were blocked by political resistance. The 2024 gazette formalised what had been de facto subordination since 1993, reducing the count from 341 to 340.

### Grassroots: The Grama Niladhari Division

Beneath the DSD sits the **Grama Niladhari Division (GND)** — the smallest state administrative unit, staffed by an appointed Grama Niladhari officer responsible for household enumeration, certificates, and dispute reporting. The designation evolved from Village Headman → Grama Sevaka → Grama Seva Niladhari → Grama Niladhari. The number of GNDs grew from 4,451 in 1987 to over 14,000 today, driven partly by the absorption of Special Service Officers (1987), cultivation officers (1988), and agriculture extension officers (1989) into the Grama Niladhari system.

## Summary: A Three-Layer Inheritance

| Level | Count | When Established | Primary Driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| Provinces | 9 | 1833–1889 (colonial); 1987 (devolution) | Colonial geography; post-war ethnic politics |
| Districts | 25 | 1955 (20 original); 1959–1984 (+5) | Development planning; ethnic demography |
| DSDs | 340 | 1992 (formal); ongoing splits | Welfare state delivery; population growth; politics |

Sri Lanka's administrative geography is thus a palimpsest: colonial lines never erased, post-independence additions layered over them, and an ongoing process of subdivision driven by population pressure, service demand, and political economy. The province boundaries drawn by two British commissioners in 1833 still define the outer frame; the district boundaries drawn in 1955 still define the planning tier; and the DSDs, the youngest layer, keep splitting as the state reaches further down into villages and households.
